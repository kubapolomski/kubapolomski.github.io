EasySend - Business Panel

FinTech

Overview

At EasySend, our business clients had their own version of the Dashboard. The main difference between individual and business apps was the Wallet - the currency accounts. Our clients could top-up the Wallet in their native currency and exchange it in seconds, with a minimum fee. Then they could pay like the local - e.g., for invoices in foreign currency.

As our Customer Service was getting a few negative reviews after releasing the Business Panel, I was asked to investigate the cause of the unenthusiastic feedback.

Team

  • Product Owner
  • Product Designer (me)
  • Front-end developer (as a consultant)

Deliverables

  • Usability test report
  • Lo-fi sketches
  • Hi-Fi mock-ups
  • Prototypes

The process

The Process

1. Research

I started by observing user behavior with tools like HotJar and Google Analytics. I quickly realized that many of our users had problems navigating the Dashboard. There was a lot of running in circles. Sometimes they're just jumping between every single item in the navigation system to find the information they're looking for.

I wanted to gather more information, so I decided to run usability tests.

The Process

2. Usability tests

I ran sessions with a few of our existing clients and few potential clients - owners of a business who haven't used our service yet but could do that in the future.

After that, I knew what caused these problems, and now I had to find solutions for them.

the process

3. Ideation

At first, I focused on the idea of the onboarding - explaining how the Business Panel works and how to operate it. That should do.

Other ideas included providing access to a Demo account and contextual helpers.

Then I realized that it was a too easy solution. Easy for us - fast to deliver, simple to implement - but not much better experience for our users.
I decided to go with redesigning the whole Information Architecture as it will produce much better results.

I quickly jumped into Sketch and started rewriting the IA. While doing that, I've also started working on other things, which we identified as little confusing in usability tests.

The Results

Besides changing only IA, I've also added a few minor changes, like changing exchange rates chart to more pleasing one, and adding clear call-to-action to every component that needed it.

outcomes

  • Much easier to understand Information Architecture (menu without accordion) - users immediately knew what to do
  • Responsive menu (can be partially hidden so that users can have more real-estate)
  • Badges (webhooks) on items that require user action
  • Actionable components ("Deposit" buttons on Wallets, Buy or Sell on exchange rate chart module)
