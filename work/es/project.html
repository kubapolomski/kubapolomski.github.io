EasySend Mobile Apps

FinTech

Overview

As the EasySend International Transfers grew, they decided to add mobile apps to their existing product (till then, they only had web application). As soon as I joined the company, I was asked to transfer web experience to the mobile apps (iOS and Android), while improving user-flows where I could, with as least changes in business logic as possible (same API for web and mobile apps).

Team

  • Product Owner
  • Business Analyst
  • Product Designer (me)
  • 2 iOS & 2 Android developers (external company)
  • 2 back-end developers working on API for apps (internal)

    • We were working in Scrum (Agile) framework.

    I've also worked with the Marketing Team to set brand language and style and incorporate it into our apps.

Deliverables

  • Hi-fi mock-ups (Photoshop, Sketch)

The process

1. Research

Since EasySend had no previous UX research, I had to start with that. They knew very much about their users, but mostly from a marketing perspective. It didn't tell me much about users' needs and their problems.

Methods

  • Interviews with existing EasySend users and stakeholders
  • Behavior analysis (Google Analytics, HotJar)
  • Personas
  • Surveys

I started by interviewing our users to create a real-life persona. After dozens of quick interviews over the phone, I already discovered patterns for our users - what problems and needs they had. I created 2 primary personas for whom I designed the app.

2. Ideation

App architecture

Login screens

At first, I was thinking about registration with the phone number, as the flow would be more comfortable than with email (and it's activation link), where you have to go back-and-forth between the EasySend app and Mail client.

But after reviewing ideas with the Marketing team, we decided to leave just email/ social media registration. The email was more valuable to them than a phone number, and our users reported that giving an email address is less concerning than giving phone numbers (especially before making the first transfer).

Solutions

Information Architecture

Our mobile version of the web application had quite a complicated navigation system. It was divided into two sections: one in bottom tab bars and one hidden in the hamburger menu. Therefore users had to decide which navigation to use.

I had to redesign our architecture for mobile apps, so users wouldn't have to think about where they can find different things. Based on HotJar recording, I knew that sometimes even experienced users had problems navigating our web-app.

I've decided to use the bottom navigation bar with tabs (as that was easiest to use navigation system) with 4 main items: Dashboard, Transfers, Recipients, Profile. One item in menu would be for call-to-action: Send money - right in the middle.

Later on, I decided to remove the Dashboard view because we had to cut as many things as we could for the MLP (MVP) version. You can see final IA in lives apps in Play Store or App Store.
